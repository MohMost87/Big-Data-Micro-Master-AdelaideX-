
## Assignment instructions - Part 1: Design
The first thing you should do before you start looking for data to use -- and before you start coding -- is to design your program. You should have done this several times before now, and it’s no different in your major assignment. 

Your design should include:

* a statement of what your data is
* the analysis you want to perform, and
* how the data will be displayed.

Additionally, you should decompose the problem into functions, writing the function signature for each one you will need.

Remember: a function signature indicates the return type, function name, and any function parameters.

Some additional drawings can be made of your final visualisation. That will be the general design of how you will finally display your data. You should include graphical displays as well as possibly displaying some of your raw data.

You should submit any documents or images you generate with your code at the end of this assignment

## Assignment instructions - Part 2: Data


Step 1
The first step it to find your data. The data you use is up to you, and you should find data relating to something that interests you, something that you would like to find out more about or analyse. If you are unable to find your own data, then the Australian Bureau of Meteorology has lots of meteorological data you can find and use, including  Climate Data Online.

Step 2
Once you have collected your data, you should store your data in the best way, as has been the case in every other activity and assignment. You will need to consider how you want to use your data: will you be adding and removing values? If so, maybe a variable sized array is the best option. Does it stay the same and not change? Then, you could use a static array.

After you find your data source, store it in your program.

Step 3
Another stage, depending on what data you are looking at, is to sanitise it. If you do not need all of your data, or some of your data needs to be transformed to be useful, do this at this point in the process. You sanitised the data when analysing the rainfall for a city, and you weren’t interested in days when there was no rain, so you removed those values.

## Assignment instructions - Part 3: Analysis


After collecting and storing your data, you will need to analyse it. This analysis may include many things, but at minimum, you should try to calculate:

average
maximum and minimum
standard deviation, and
mode.
These should only be done if applicable. Some forms of data, particularly if you are working with strings, may not apply.

Regardless, you will need to perform some analysis on your data -- either statistical, or other applicable forms. For example, if your data source is the text of a book, you may want to count the most frequent words, or see how similar each paragraph is to another. 

Processing Functions

As this is your final assignment, you may use any in-built Process functions taht you would like, although it it always good practice to implement some of the more common data analysis functions yourself, to get more experience dealing with data and functions.



## Assignment instructions - Part 4: Visualisation


When you have finished your analysis, you will need some method for visualising your data and the analysis you have performed. In this course so far, you have seen examples of bar graphs and pie charts.

You should find a unique method of displaying your data. It should be meaningful to what your data relates to. It should also make your data and the analysis easy to understand.

If you are stuck trying to visualise your data, then the Information is Beautiful website may provide you with some ideas.

Some requirements of your visualisation are that the data visualisation can scale and still be visualised correctly. This means if your data values change, the visualisation should still be fully displayed on the canvas, and no data should overlap, or be missing. Additionally, the canvas should be able to change size without changing the visualisation.
